The Thermogenic Resistance Discovery

Thermogenesis is the body's natural heat-generating process that burns calories and fat as a byproduct of normal metabolic function — digestion, movement, body temperature regulation, and cellular energy production. When thermogenesis is functioning properly, the body maintains a baseline calorie burn that keeps weight in check even without intense exercise.

The problem: research now shows that thermogenesis declines significantly after age 35 — particularly in women, but affecting both sexes. The body progressively becomes resistant to the metabolic signals that should trigger thermogenesis. This is what researchers call thermogenic resistance — and it explains a phenomenon most adults over 35 have experienced personally: eating the same diet and exercising the same way as ten years ago, yet gaining weight steadily regardless.

🔬 The Seville Orange Breakthrough

Studies show p-synephrine activates beta-3 adrenergic receptors, which trigger thermogenesis without raising blood pressure or heart rate like ephedrine. The research shows thermogenesis can be increased by up to 74% — meaning the body burns significantly more fat continuously, including during sleep.
